## Design Context

### Users
Dispatch is for solo founders and indie hackers who ship product work regularly and want to build in public on X, but lose momentum because every commit creates a blank-page writing task. They are usually working alone, moving fast, and trying to turn visible progress into trust without maintaining a separate content calendar.

The core job is simple: connect GitHub and X, push a meaningful commit, review 2-3 generated build-in-public drafts, optionally edit a word, and post. The emotional job is to remove the "what do I even say?" moment while keeping the final post personal enough that the builder would put their own name under it.

### Brand Personality
Dispatch should feel sharp, honest, and momentum-building.

It should speak like a focused builder who respects the user's time: direct, concrete, lightly opinionated, and allergic to generic AI-social-media language. The product should create relief and confidence first, then a small flash of pride when a dull commit becomes a post that sounds worth sharing.

### Aesthetic Direction
The visual direction is disciplined indie utility: light-first, high-contrast, mostly black and white, with one restrained accent used for action and signal. It should feel like a serious workbench for people who ship, not a glossy social media automation suite.

The landing page can be sharper and more editorial, but the app workspace should stay quiet, dense, and efficient. The memorable moment should come from the commit-to-draft transformation: real commit context becoming three distinct post angles. Avoid generic SaaS gradients, glassy AI dashboards, neon-on-dark "developer tool" styling, and anything that makes Dispatch feel like a content calendar.

### Design Principles
1. Make the commit-to-draft transformation the hero. Every major surface should reinforce that Dispatch turns real shipping activity into postable build-in-public updates.
2. Optimize for momentum. Interfaces should reduce decisions, keep primary actions obvious, and make the next step feel close.
3. Protect the user's voice. Copy and UI should frame generated drafts as builder-owned, reviewable, and worth posting, not as disposable AI content.
4. Stay visually disciplined. Use black, white, and one accent with strong hierarchy, precise spacing, and minimal decoration.
5. Keep the core path polished. Pay, connect, review, edit, attach image, and post must feel reliable and deliberate; everything outside the core can remain plain.

## Answers Derived From The Docs

1. Brand words: sharp, honest, momentum-building.
2. Desired feeling: relief from the blank page, confidence that the draft is post-worthy, and momentum to keep the build log alive.
3. References implied by the docs: practical indie-builder tools and direct founder-led products, especially those that privilege speed, clarity, and proof over broad marketing polish. The docs do not name specific reference sites.
4. Anti-references: generic AI social-media generators, changelog-to-post wrappers, content-calendar products, glossy automation dashboards, and anything that sounds like "AI content" instead of a builder sharing progress.
5. Visual direction: disciplined indie utility with a sharper editorial landing page; minimal, direct, and opinionated rather than playful or luxurious.
6. Theme: light-first. Dark mode can exist for app comfort later, but the launch direction is white background, black text, one accent.
7. Color constraints: black/white/one accent only. The accent should signal CTA and status sparingly; avoid multi-color palettes, purple-blue AI gradients, neon developer styling, and decorative color systems.
